Definitions
Conflict area A ‘conflict area’ is the area of the intersection that is limited by the first nodes of
ingress / egress vehicle lanes, first nodes of ‘ingress path’ crosswalk lanes and stop
lines of bicycle lanes. For a better understanding, see also e.g. Figure G.6 in [ISO/TS
19091 2019-06].
Merge point As all attributes are provided in the order of the nodes, a ‘merge point’ designates
a node of a lane where the lane is split into two lanes in driving direction towards
an intersection (ingress). On the other hand, a merge point on an egress lane is
located, where two lanes end in one lane in driving direction. The opposite applies
for ‘diverge points’.
Traffic Dependent Refers to an operation mode of the traffic light controller that dynamically adapts
the changes to the current traffic situation, i.e. the cycle of the traffic phases is not
static but may change over time in contrast to pre-timed programs. The term
“actuated” is also used as a synonym.
Operational A traffic light is considered ‘operational’, if the corresponding traffic light controller
is neither switched off nor in any kind of failure mode. This means that also traffic
lights showing some kind of “standby” (e.g. at night) are considered operational.
Phase A general term denoting all the movement phase states strictly allowing or
prohibiting to proceed into an intersection (so the “Reds” and “Greens” as
summarized in [SAE J2735]).
Transition A general term denoting all the movement phase states which are not covered by
the term phase except for ‘unavailable’ and ‘dark’ (so the “Yellows / Ambers” as
summarized in [SAE J2735]).
Phase state A general term that covers all movement phase states as defined in [SAE J2735], i.e.
‘phase state’ includes both phases and transitions.
Confidence level Information provided with a ‘confidence level’ of 95% means that the true value is
inside the provided value range (or confidence area) for at least 95% of the data
points in a given statistical base.

1.1 C-Roads platform for harmonisation of C-ITS
deployment
The C-Roads Platform is a joint initiative of European Member States and road operators for testing and
implementing C-ITS services in light of cross-border harmonisation and interoperability. Through the C-Roads
Platform, authorities and road operators join together to harmonise the deployment activities of cooperative
intelligent transport systems (C-ITS) across Europe. The goal is to achieve the deployment of interoperable
cross-border C-ITS services for road users.
C-ITS enables vehicles to interact directly with each other and the surrounding road infrastructure. In road
transport, C-ITS typically involves vehicle-to-vehicle (V2V) and vehicle-to-infrastructure (V2I) communication.
In order to enable an efficient and undisturbed exchange of information within these services as well as a
cross-border implementation, harmonised C-ITS specifications are indispensable. The approach starts from a
functional perspective, then requirements applicable to all implementations and then towards technology
specifications of currently validated implementations (ITS-G5 for short range communication, IP based for long
range cellular). In order to meet these challenges, the C-ROADS platform is divided into five Working Groups.
The first Working Group is concerned with organisational tasks, the second with Technical Aspects and the
third with Evaluation and Assessment. The fourth Working Group is about Urban C-ITS Harmonisation and
Working Group 5 is about Digital Transport Infrastructure (DTI).
The C-Roads Platform is steered by the C-Roads Steering Committee which is composed by Member State
representatives. With the support of the Supporting Secretariat, decisions for achieving the goal of the
implementation of interoperable end-user services are taken. In this respect specifications, plans and reports,
which are proposed and recommended by specific Working Groups, are approved. Within WG2 these
specifications are harmonized in 5 Task Forces and derived from pilot activities and the basis for further pilot
and implementation activities. This especially goes with technical decisions, which influence deployment and
procurement decisions at pilot sites. The Working Groups are installed as decision support for the Steering
Committee to ensure proper decisions towards interoperable deployments. Individual experts participating in
the single pilots work together in these Working Groups to prepare proposals and recommendations. Also,
members of the single pilot activities as well as of the C-Roads-Working Groups actively contribute to the work
of the EU-C-ITS-Platform.

This introductory paragraph explains the motivation and scope of this guideline as well as the targeted

1.3.1 Motivation
The need for such a guideline in addition to existing standards and profiles arises from the need of harmonized
implementation of MAPEM and SPATEM on the one hand and the innumerable amount of different
intersection setups and configurations on the other hand.
Every intersection is unique and some general procedures and setups differ among the different European
countries, every single one of them bringing new challenges for traffic engineers. Many design decisions on
SPATEM / MAPEM will require a case-by-case decision. However, the implementation of SPATEM and MAPEM
needs to be harmonized throughout Europe such that vehicle applications work reliably and consistently
everywhere. The existing profiles and standards leave room for adaptation to specific situations, but a
harmonized, extensive guidance how to conduct these adaptations is not available. This document intends to
fill that void for informative guidelines for traffic engineers in order to ensure true harmonization of SPATEM
and MAPEM in Europe. In addition to providing guidance in the message creation, this document also provides
some explanations and reasoning why certain design choices were made in the standards and profiles.
1.3.2 Scope
The document at hand applies to the services “Road and Lane Topology” (RLT) and “Traffic Light Manoeuvre”
(TLM) as defined by ETSI. Both services can be deployed for different scenarios. The guidelines provided in this
document focus solely on the implementation for signalled intersections. See below the service definitions for
RLT and TLM as defined in ETSI:
Road and Lane Topology (RLT) as defined in [ETSI TS 103 301], V1.3.1 (2020-02):
“The RLT is one instantiation of the infrastructure services to manage the generation, transmission and
reception of a digital topological map, which defines the topology of an infrastructure area. It includes the
lane topology for e.g. vehicles, bicycles, parking, public transportation and the paths for pedestrian crossings
and the allowed manoeuvres within an intersection area or a road segment. In future enhancements, the
digital map will include additional topology-descriptions like traffic roundabouts.”
Traffic Light Manoeuvre (TLM) as defined in [ETSI TS 103 301], V1.3.1 (2020-02):
“The TLM is one instantiation of the infrastructure services to manage the generation, transmission and
reception of SPATEM messages. The TLM includes safety-related information for supporting traffic participants
(vehicles, pedestrians, etc.) to execute safe manoeuvres in an intersection area. The goal is to enter and exit
an intersection "conflict area" in a controlled way. The TLM informs in real-time about the operational states
of the traffic light controller, the current signal state, the residual time of the state before changing to the next
state, the allowed manoeuvres and aids with crossing. Additionally, the TLM FLS foresees the inclusion of
detailed green way advisory information and the status for public transport prioritization.”

2. MAPEM and SPATEM landscape
2.1 Use cases
The C-Roads Common C-ITS Service and Use Case Definitions [C-Roads SUD] describe C-ITS services and use
cases in a functional way. Next to these functional descriptions it contains per use case the generic reference
to specific other C-Roads WG2 documentation and it contains the harmonized specific use case settings to
reach interoperability. One section of this document deals with Signalised Intersections.
The Signalised Intersection service will provide information to road users, and vehicle data to C-ITS stations,
for a safe and efficient approach and crossing of a signalised intersection(s). The implementation of the
infrastructure-based intersection use cases will increase the safety and traffic flow efficiency and minimize
environmental pollution at a signalised intersection. The following use cases are included in [C-Roads SUD]
(here: version 2.0.0):
• Signal Phase and Timing Information;
• Green Light Optimal Speed Advisory;
• Imminent Signal Violation Warning;
• Traffic Light Prioritisation;
• Emergency Vehicle Priority.
2.2 Interrelation of MAPEM and SPATEM
The MapData (MAPEM) message ([SAE J2735], [ISO/TS19091]) is used to convey many types of geographic
road information. At the current time, its primary use is to convey one or more intersection lane geometry
maps within a single message. The map message content includes such items as complex intersection
descriptions, road segment descriptions, high speed curve outlines (used in curve safety messages), and
segments of roadway (used in some safety applications). A given single MapData message may convey
descriptions of one or more geographic areas or intersections. The contents of this message provide a
topological basis that in turn is used by other messages to relate additional information (for example, the
signal phase and timing via the Signal Phase and Timing Extended Message (SPATEM)) to events at specific
geographic locations on the roadway. The SPATEM message is used to convey the current signalling status of
one or more signalized intersections. Along with the MapData message (which describes a full geometric
layout of an intersection) the receiver of this message can determine the state of the signal phasing and when
the next expected phase will occur, subject to its geographical position on the intersection.
Common practice in C-ITS deployment in Europe today is that a MAPEM contains the lane geometry of a single
physical intersection as controlled by one traffic light controller (as opposed to multiple physical intersection
e.g. on a corridor controlled by multiple traffic light controllers).

2.3.1 Update and versioning
For every intersection, it is important, that the information on the road topology and the corresponding
signalling information are consistent and match the actual situation at the intersection at any time. For
MAPEM and SPATEM this means that correct versioning of the two messages is crucial.
To give an example: if an intersection is changed e.g. by adding a separate lane for a right turn where before
there only was one combined lane for going straight and making a right turn, this needs to be reflected in a
corresponding update of MAPEM. For every update in MAPEM it needs to be ensured, that the SPATEM
information is consistent with this update. The same holds of course for any kind of change or update relevant
for SPATEM or MAPEM in the road topology or traffic planning.
Therefore, the correct handling of versioning needs to be handled through the whole chain of:
• Planning of traffic application;
• Generation of MAPEM;
• Archiving for version counting and provision to different channels;
• Transport over different communication channels;
• Layering and merging;
• Verification of the MAPEM;
• Validation of SPATEM and MAPEM.
In both MAPEM and SPATEM there is a corresponding revision number contained, representing the version or
release number of the messages. Vehicles will check if both messages have the same revision and discard the
messages if their revisions do not match.
2.3.2 Fragmentation for MAPEM
A MAPEM can become quite large, especially for complex intersections. Since the amount of data per single
message frame is limited to 1398 bytes [ETSI EN 302 636-4-1], it can become necessary to fragment the
MAPEM (i.e., provide a single MAPEM fragmented in multiple transmissions). This fragmentation is done on
the application layer. To identify the individual parts belonging to one single MAPEM, the data element
“layerID” is used.
If possible, an intersection should be contained entirely in a single fragment / in a single message. If that is not
possible, at least all lanes of an approach should be contained in the same fragment. Further fragmentation
rules are defined in ISO 19091 section G.8.3.2 on DE_LayerID.
In any case, an individual lane shall not be separated among multiple fragments but always be fully contained
in a single fragment. In addition, duplication of data shall be avoided (e.g., providing one approach in multiple
fragments) to reduce channel load.
2.3.3 Safety assurance
Vehicle applications based on MAPEM and SPATEM information are relevant for traffic safety. Therefore, a
receiver shall be able to validate the completeness, the consistency and the versions of the MAPEM and
SPATEM not only through the revision but also through other measures. For example, using a securing element
like CRC or signature within the MAPEM should be discussed for the future.

2.4 Precision, message size and quality
As intersections can become very complex, the MAPEM message tends to be a very large message. In practical
cases when transmitted over a medium with a limited maximum data size per message like 802.11p (1500
bytes), the MAPEM must be split over multiple messages (layers). Generally, to reduce the use of bandwidth
of the transmission, the size of the MAPEM should be as small as possible, while maintaining the required
quality.
Ne
A major part of the MAPEM is taken by the nodes that describe the layout of the lanes or the connections
between the lanes. Therefore, it is advised to reduce the number of nodes as much as possible. There are
quality requirements in the C-Roads profile that put a limit on the reduction of the number of nodes. The
following list contains the corresponding parameters as defined in the C-Roads profile:
• the minimum length of an ingress lane, pMinIngressLaneLength;
• the minimum length of an egress lane, pMinEgressLaneLength;
• the absolute lateral offset of node points to the centre of the lane, pLateralNodeOffset;
• the perpendicular distance between the linear connection of two node points and the centre of the lane,
pMaxPerpendDistLaneCenter.
This is reflected by the maximum number of nodes allowed per lane that has been set to a lower value than
the MAPEM encoding allows. The number of nodes of a lane may exceed that number to keep the
perpendicular distance between the linear connection of two node points and the centre of the lane below
the defined threshold, while still fulfilling the required minimum lane length.
The actual values of for the lateral offset and the perpendicular distance mentioned above have been set to
relatively large values when compared to the commonly used lane width. This is justified by the limited
absolute accuracy of the current GNSS systems and intersection documentation available. Taking an
opportunistic view, one could reduce nodes by placing nodes at the maximum allowed distance to the centre
of the lanes. Even as this is allowed, there are some rules of thumb that increase the usability of the MAPEM
message now and in the future:
• Nodes must be placed close to the lane centre lines of the base material. Rationale: given the limited
absolute accuracy of for example a CAD drawing, the lateral offset specified in the C-Roads profile is
needed to cover for this inaccuracy. Adding the allowed offset to the possible offset in the base material
could place the node outside the allowed offset in the real world.
• Lateral node placement must reflect the logic of the road topology, e.g. lanes must be a lane width apart.
Rationale: even though the absolute accuracy might not be very high, the relative distances between
lanes is often much more accurate. Vehicles might compensate for an absolute error in the location but
cannot do so for the relative distances in the MAPEM.

3.1 LayerID
For more complex intersections, MAPEM can become too large for a single packet. MAPEM offers the
possibility for fragmentation (multiple fragments / packets for one MAPEM). If more than one message is
needed, the LayerIDs in each message help receivers to understand that the message is only one fragment of
a larger MAPEM. For example, if two MapData messages are needed, the LayerID of the first is set to 21, and
the second to 22, thus indicating the first and second part of a two-part MAPEM. If the complete topology fits
into one MapData message, this field shall not be used.
The receiver shall be able to assemble the messages. The revision number of the MAPEM assures which
fragments belong together. Validation of MAPEM shall be done on the message level, not the fragment level.
Therefore, the concrete fragmentation of the message (if necessary) can be decided case by case since other
(logical) requirements could not be guaranteed to be applicable in all situations.

Refers to mapData.intersections

situations, only one intersection will be defined in a MAPEM. The details how to represent an intersection in
MAPEM are explained in the following sections.

Refers to mapData.intersections.name

3.2.3 Revision
Refers to mapData.intersections.revision
The revision number serves as a versioning number for the MAPEM information. If the topology information
for an intersection change e.g., additional lanes are built, lanes are rearranged or similar, the MAPEM needs
to be updated according to this new information. The revision data element then needs to be incremented
according to the profile. The detailed processes and conditions when and how to increment the revision are
out of the scope of this document. If the maximum value available is reached, the revision starts over with 0
for the next version of the message.
It is very important, that the revision numbers of MAPEM and SPATEM for one intersection are identical. This
way, receiving vehicles can check if MAPEM and SPATEM are consistent (see also section 2.2). Furthermore,
vehicles could have a local memory of known MAPEMs. Upon reception of a MAPEM they can check through
the intersection reference ID and the revision, whether the MAPEM is already known or needs to be re-
evaluated.
3.2.4 RefPoint
Refers to mapData.intersections.refPoint
As described in the basic intersection composition in section 4.1.2, each intersection contains a reference
point roughly at the centre of an intersection (conflict area), see Figure 1. The geographical proximity of the
reference position to the actual centre of the intersection has several advantages: on the one hand, this leads
to the fact that the delta positions are smaller and thus require less space in the message. On the other hand,
this way receiving vehicles can filter relevant intersections on a first level by their reference positions, before
having to evaluate the entire message contents.

3.3 LaneSet
Refers to mapData.intersections.laneSet
The laneSet [DF_LaneList] data frame provides information on all lanes of an intersection that are controlled
by light signals. Every lane is described in detail [DF_GenericLane] with its path, the approach it belongs to
(see Figure 2), reference to other lanes it is connected to, and several other attributes. The
‘multipleLanesTreatedAsOneLane’ as part of LaneSharing shall not be used to ensure that all relevant lanes
are described uniquely.
Figure 2 Intersection vehicle lanes
In general, a MAPEM shall give an adequate and comprehensive representation of the intersection’s topology.
Knowledge about all lanes of an intersection is important to properly advice the driver of potential conflict
zones and areas the driver must pay special attention (e.g., when crossing the lane of tracked vehicles or
crosswalks).
If for some intersections bicycle lanes or lanes for tracked vehicles are omitted in the MAP e.g., for message
size reasons, the vehicle is not able to differentiate whether these lanes really do not exist for this intersection
or if they are just omitted in the MAP. Additional safety assistance as described above can then not be provided
in a manner, which is comprehensible for the driver (he would not understand why a certain assistance is
provided at one signalized intersection but not at another). Therefore, the lane set is required to comprise all
traffic-light-controlled lanes of an intersection.

3.3.2 Lane attributes
The lane attribute requirements are a key aspect of understanding and implementing the scenarios provided
in this handbook. This chapter will take a deeper look into these lane attributes and provide explanations and
a guidance on how to make use of lane attributes like laneType or directionalUse.
To create a good understanding of the attributes this chapter will show some examples supported by
visualizations. Due to a huge amount of different lane attributes not all of them can be described in this
chapter. Therefore, only the most common and used lane attributes will be provided here. Nevertheless, these
examples will cover the most common intersection topologies.
3.3.2.1 LaneType
The LaneType parameter describes the main characteristics a lane can have; it is mandatory to be set. The C-
Roads Message Profile [C-Roads MP] (here: v2.0.6) states that the following laneTypes are used:
• Vehicle Lane
• crosswalkLane
• bikeLane
• sideWalk
• medianLane
• trackedVehicle
Every lane type is encoded as a bit string, providing the possibility to further detail the lanes characteristics or
intended use. For normal lanes with no further specific characteristics (e.g., revocable or restricted for special
usage), simply none of the bits is set (i.e., all bits of the respective bitstring remain 0). This applies to all
aforementioned lane types.

3.3.2.2 sharedWith
SharedWith is a parameter which describes the usage of the lane by different types of road users. The focus is
that the different road users may use the lane at the same time and therefore must share the lane. This can
happen often in traffic and therefore the sharedWith parameter is mandatory. Same as for the lane type, the
sharedWidth data element is a bit string. If a lane is not shared with other specific road users, then this bit
string remains all 0. The bits are defined as follows:
• overlappingLaneDescriptionProvided (0)
• otherNonMotorizedTrafficTypes (2)
• individualMotorizedVehicleTraffic (3)
• busVehicleTraffic (4)
• taxiVehicleTraffic (5)
• pedestriansTraffic (6)
• cyclistVehicleTraffic (7)
• trackedVehicleTraffic(8)
The bits multipleLanesTreatedAsOneLane (1) and pedestrianTraffic(9) are intentionally left out in this list.
According to the C-Roads Profile, they shall never be set (all lanes shall be described individually and sharing
with pedestrian traffic is covered by pedestrianTraffic (6) [C-Roads MP]. In the following, the bits that may be
used if applicable are explained in more detail.
The bit overlappingLaneDescriptionProvided (0) can be used when the other user type that the lane is shared
with is modelled through a separate lane with identical nodeList. This bit shall not be used when an
overlapping revocableLane of the same lane type is defined.
The following bits should only be set if the sharing of the lane requires specific attention from the primary lane
user. That means for normal vehicle lanes, where e.g., also buses and non-motorized traffic are generally
allowed to travel, these bits should not be set. Otherwise, for most normal vehicle lanes all the bits (2), (3),
(4), (5) and (7) would always be set and thus not be helpful anymore.
The bit otherNonMotorizedTrafficTypes (2) refers for example to horse drawn vehicles.
The bit individualMotorizedTrafficTypes (3) should be set for explicit tracked vehicle lanes where individual
motorized road users are also allowed to travel.
The bit busVehicleTraffic (4) on the other hand should be set for vehicle lanes that are shared with busses.
This bit might be set e.g., in cases where buses merge from an individual bus lane to the vehicle lane, since
drivers might need to be cautious there.
The same holds for the bit taxiVehicleTraffic (5).
The bit trackedVehicleTraffic (8) should be set for vehicle lanes on which tracked vehicles also are allowed
travel.
The bit pedestrianTraffic (6) should be set e.g., on bike lanes on which pedestrians also explicitly are allowed
to walk.
The bit cyclistVehicleTraffic (7) should be used for vehicle lanes that are explicitly shared with bicycles
(indicated through corresponding road markings and/or traffic signs) and for sideWalks where cyclists are also
allowed to travel.
The decision which road user to use for the lane type and which road user to indicate in the sharedWith bits
(e.g., is it a vehicle lane shared with tracked vehicles or a tracked vehicle lane shared with individual motorized

3.3.4.4 Merge points and diverge points
When lanes merge/diverge or when multiple lanes must be connected to each other, a
mergePoint/divergePoint is present and the overlapping nodes of all affected lanes must have the attribute
mergePoint/divergePoint assigned.
Hereby it is important that the diverge or merge points in both lanes represent the same absolute position
(down to a certain accuracy). The C-Roads Profile requires a rather high accuracy for this, so that receivers can
easily identify which lanes merge or diverge. To achieve this high accuracy, a simple calculation principle can
be used, there is no additional measuring effort needed. The calculation principle basically consists of
computing the cumulative node offset positions of the preceding nodes relative to the reference point. This
cumulative offset is then subtracted from the offset between the merge / diverge point’s absolute position
and the reference point. Thus, it can be made sure, that the sum of all node offsets results in the correct
absolute position of the merge / diverge point in both lanes. See also Figure 8 and the formulas for better
understanding.

3.3.5 ConnectsTo
Refers to mapData.intersections.laneSet.connectsTo
This data frame contains a list of connection [DF_Connection] data frames. This list of connections describes

manoeuvre applies to instead of only listing the possible manoeuvres of a lane. Side effect is that with this

phase information in SPATEM applies to the respective connection. Therefore, it is essential that every given
‘signalGroup’ / ‘intersectionReferenceID’ tuple in the MAPEM is also contained in the SPATEM.
It is important to note, that there is not necessarily a 1:1 relation between physical signal heads on the street
and connections/signalGroup e.g. if a connection is controlled by 2 signals, their combined state shall be
reflected in the eventState (see section 5.7.3).
One exception to this combination of states is a situation when multiple user classes share a lane, and each
user class is served with separate signals (e.g., public transport). In such a case, a lane may have multiple
(overlapping) connections, each with a unique signalGroup and the userClass restriction set.

4.1.2 Reference point
The reference point is crucial for every intersection layout. It is the fix point of origin for the entire intersection
topology represented in MAPEM, meaning that all other location information in the message is given in
coordinates relative to the reference point. Therefore, the accuracy and precision of the reference point is a
significant factor for the accuracy of all topology information.
C-Roads’ specifications require that the reference point is located roughly at the centre of the conflict area.
This may seem quite vague but is sufficient and intentional [C-Roads MP].
For receiving vehicles (and also for testing purposes) it is important to have the reference point located within
the conflict area (for a clear, topological relation to the actual intersection). Positioning of the reference point
precisely at the conflict area’s centre however is not necessary. The reference point itself doesn’t need to be
measured exactly. It could be calculated from another, exactly measured point nearby (e.g., at the border of
the conflict area) as long as the overall accuracy requirements for the topology information are still fulfilled.
For more details on the corresponding data field in MAPEM, see section 3.2.4.

4.4 Adjacent intersection lane connections
Intersections that are within a short distance of each other can be provided with a single MAPEM. To achieve
this the basic principles for egress and ingress lanes remain:
• An egress lane must be provided for all outbound lanes;
• Egress lanes of one intersection shall not overlap ingress lanes of an adjacent intersection (minimum
egress lane lengths must be respected and as a consequence the pMinIngressLaneLength cannot be
achieved);
• If the adjacent intersection is not V2X-equipped (or not transmitting MAPEM and SPATEM for any other
reason), still the last node of any lane in MAPEM of the intersection at hand shall be distanced at least
pMinEgressLaneLength (see [C-Roads MP], here: v2.0.6) from the adjacent intersection’s conflict area.
Figure 36 shows the configuration of one MAPEM with two intersections (A and B) within a short distance of
each other. The use of the remoteIntersection data element is discouraged to ensure that ‘complete’
intersection topologies are provided i.e., with both ingress and egress lanes for all intersections within the

4.8 Bicycle lane configurations
Different configurations for bike lanes exist, including different types of lane markings, lane sharing rules and
longitudinal configuration changes. Typically, three different road layouts can be distinguished:
• Lanes shared by vehicles and cyclists, without dedicated bicycle pavement or road markings that
demarcate the bicycle lane. For these configurations the sharedWith (cyclistVehicleTraffic) data element
should be used.
• Lanes shared by vehicle and cyclist, with dedicated bicycle pavement or road markings with
discontinuous lane markings that demarcate the bicycle lane. For these configurations the
segmentAttributeXY adjacentBikeLaneOnLeft/adjacentBikeLaneOnRight should be used in addition to
the sharedWith (cyclistVehicleTraffic) data element.
• Lanes with dedicated space reserved for cyclists, typically demarcated by a continuous white line and/or
coloured pavement. For these configurations the bicycle lanes should be provided as individual lanes.
The adjacent vehice lane should have the segmentAttributeXY
adjacentBikeLaneOnLeft/adjacentBikeLaneOnRight set.
• Infrastructures with bicycle facilities physically separated from vehicle lanes. For these configurations
the cycling facilities are provided as unique bikeLanes.
Bicycle lanes that are signalised separately from vehicle lanes should always be provided as separate lanes, to
allow for the provision of separate connections and signal groups.
4.8.1 Bicycle lane with discontinuous lane markings
Bicycle lanes with discontinuous lane marking, where lane-sharing is present, should be modelled by the lane
that is also used for other vehicles. The element sharedWith should contain cyclistVehicleTraffic (7) and the
laneType should be set to vehicle. The segment attribute adjacentBikeLaneOnLeft / adjacentBikeLaneOnRight
should be used.

4.9 Multiple intersections
Some large or very complex intersections should be modelled as multiple individual intersections. Such
examples and explanations are provided in this chapter – for exceptionally large intersections and for multiple
intersections including bicycle lanes.
4.9.1 Exceptionally large intersections
In case of exceptionally large intersections or configurations that logically consist of multiple intersections, the
IntersectionGeometry can be created by distinguishing independent conflict areas. These are defined as:
• A conflict area having own stop lines and signal heads for all conflicting directions.
• A conflict area that – theoretically – can be controlled independently from other conflict areas safely.
• A conflict area does not share the conflict matrix with another conflict area.
One motivation for creating one IntersectionGeometry for each independent conflict area is the limited array
size of several data elements (e.g., lanes) as defined by the standards. Note that MapData aims to objectively
describe the IntersectionGeometry as it can be observed. How the conflict areas are controlled functionally
and how they are consequently grouped, is a different perspective that is not needed by road users. This
guideline also applies to the examples provided in chapter 9.3 of the German guidance for MAP creation [OCA
DiMAP Leitfaden].

4.10.1 Unsignalized roundabouts

4.11 Stop lines and do not block segments
The first node of an ingress lane, which is signalised and/or not a diverge or merge point, shall be the node
that shall not be passed by a vehicle when unless movement is not allowed (from regulations, typically this is

4.12 Inclusion of non-priority roads
Generally, it is recommended not to include non-priority roads in the MAPEM implementation. In many cases,
vehicles wouldn’t make use of the intersection information while traveling on the non-priority road anyway
since they encounter a situation where they have to give way before reaching the signalized intersection.
If the traffic light controller needs information on side roads around the intersection (e.g., due to the presence
of sensors, to estimate traffic demand or estimate time of arrival of priority vehicles), corresponding MAPEMs
with a full representation of the side roads can be used internally at the controller. For V2X transmission the
smaller MAPEM without the side roads should be used.
There might however be situations where a transmission of MAPEM including some side roads is needed e.g.,
when public transport vehicles travel on such side roads and should make a request to the traffic light via V2X.
If the vehicles don’t have an internal map containing the information where to start the request, they need
the MAPEM including side roads. To still make sure that other individual traffic vehicles can differentiate the
lanes on minor roads from other ingress lanes (e.g., to avoid showing time to green on the side road when the
driver should still give way to the main road), an implementation as shown in Figure 58 should be used.
The side road should be assigned to the same approach ID as the ingress lanes on the main road. The ingress
on the main road is separated into two lanes. For an explanation how to link two such lanes, see sections
3.3.4.4 and 3.3.4.7. The minor road is then included to this ingress by adding a connection of lane 23 to lane
17 without any signal group indication.
Figure 58 Inclusion of non-priority road in MAPEM using connections
Through this implementation (an ingress lane that connects to another ingress lane of the same intersection),
receiving vehicles can deduce that lane 23 in this example represents a minor road and can control the
information given to the driver or vehicle systems accordingly.

4.13 Dynamic lane configuration
4.13.1 Different connections allowed depending on the time of day
Figure 59 Dynamic lane example, different connections depending on the time of day
Dynamic lanes in an intersection are configured as overlaying lanes of the same physical lane such as lane 16
and lane 18 in the example shown above. In Figure 59, lane 16 and its connection are shown in red and lane
18 and its connection are shown in yellow. During the morning peak on weekdays, i.e. from 06:30 to 10:30,
the left turn is allowed and the straight turn is not allowed. During all other times, the left turn is not allowed
and the straight turn is allowed. All lanes are present in MAPEM at all times. For vehicles to be able to identify
which representation of a lane in MAPEM is the currently valid one, the “enabledLanes” data field in SPATEM
is used. This is a set of lane IDs for all the lanes that have the “isVehicleRevocableLane” bit set and that are
valid at the time of transmission of the SPATEM. If a lane has the “isVehicleRevocableLane” bit set but its ID
doesn’t appear in the “enabledLanes” in SPATEM, it is not considered to be part of the current intersection
topology.
Through this mechanism, whenever the topology is changed, the MAPEM can remain unchanged and only the
SPATEM needs to be updated.

4.13.3 Bascule bridge / foldable bridge
The mechanism described for dynamic lane configuration is also applicable for foldable bridges (i.e., bridges
where the bridge itself is folded or lifted to make space for passing ships). Such a bridge can for example be
found in Hamburg (Mahatma-Gandhi-Brücke). In such scenarios, the corresponding part of the intersection is
not available for driving anymore. This information should also be provided in MAPEM. For example, in
situations when an emergency vehicle approaches from behind this can be helpful. At normal intersections
the vehicles could then, if necessary, enter the conflict area to give way. This is not possible if that part of the
road is currently not available for driving at all. Of course, vehicle sensors of automated vehicles should detect
that there is no road, but having the information in advance in the messages is helpful in this situation.
As stated before, here also the “isVehicleRevocableLane” and the “enabledLanes” data elements can be used.
Typically, a traffic light is located before the bridge, i.e., the egress lanes can be placed on or behind the bridge.
These egress lanes should have the “isVehicleRevocableLane” bit set. As long as the bridge allows passage, the
lane ID shall be indicated in the “enabledLanes” list in SPATEM. As soon as the bridge rises or folds, this lane

5.4 Status
Refers to signalPhaseAndTimingMessage.intersections.status
The data element ‘status’ [DE_IntersectionStatusObject] provides information about the operational status of

Refers to signalPhaseAndTimingMessagen.intersections.moy
The SPATEM contains information about phase state changes in the format of 1/10th of a second within the

Refers to signalPhaseAndTimingMessagen.intersections.timestamp

time change details where determined). That way, these time stamps serve as a basis for the evaluation of the
time change details.

A signal group, which is present in SPATEM but not in MAPEM is of no use, because the information cannot be
mapped to a corresponding connection. A signal group present in MAPEM but not in SPATEM is of no use

Additionally, a signal group being present in only one of the two messages could be interpreted on vehicle

In message generation, it should be avoided to create multiple signal groups which always provide the same
movement state information (duplicates). Multiple ingress lanes observing the same (identical) traffic light(s)
should have the same signalGroup. This is to avoid sending multiple signalGroups with the very same content
all the time. If some signal groups have the same states at some hours of the day and differ during other times
(e.g., night-time vs. rush hour), there is however no need to build different SPATEMs and MAPEMs for the
different times of day. In such cases, “part-time duplicate” movement states are acceptable.
5.7.2 State-time-speed
Refers to signalPhaseAndTimingMessage.intersections.states.state-time-speed
The state-time-speed component provides the essential information on the signal phases and phase changes
for a signal group.
According to profiling, the movement phase states shall be sorted in order of their appearance at the traffic
light. The reasoning for this is to make the structure of the message as intuitive as possible. Additionally, it
eases interpretation on vehicle side, the vehicle doesn’t have to parse through all available movement states
for one signal group but can read them from the message according to its function’s needs one by one in the
correct order.
For applications such as GLOSA it is important to not only know the current phase but also the next phase and
its duration in order to give proper advice to the driver. In short: the vehicle needs to know the time-to-green
or time-to-red (depending on the current phase).
In some countries there are transitions between two phases (e.g., yellow or red-yellow). In that case, in sum
three MovementPhaseStates shall be included in SPATEM (current phase, transition and next phase). This is
also the reason why the two terms ‘phase’ and ‘transition’ have been introduced, to make this distinction
clearer and allow for generic requirements applicable in all countries.
It is important to note that the event states don’t necessarily provide a 1:1 representation of the physical
traffic lights but rather of the applicable traffic rules as indicated by the traffic lights. One reason for this is
that some phase states are country-dependent (e.g., green blinking or yellow blinking). In general, the vehicle
needs to know the rules it needs to apply to and not the physical representation – which could have different
meanings depending on the country.
The following sections provide more detailed information on how to represent the different signal states and
the corresponding timing information.

The movement phase states are not named after specific signal colours but have denominations that are more

5.7.4.1 MinEndTime
Refers to signalPhaseAndTimingMessage.intersections.states.state-time-speed.timing.minEndTime
As the name already indicates, minEndTime shall represent the earliest time possible at which the signal phase
state can change. Except for forces majeures (such as any kind of failure) the transmitted minEndTime shall
take into account unpredictable events such as pedestrian or public transport requests and shall not move to
an earlier point in time over several SPATEMs.
For actuated traffic light controllers this might mean that minEndTime simply reflects the current time plus
the system time needed for a signal change. This may appear as a non-information but correctly reflects reality,
and a correct reflection of the real situation shall always be provided to vehicles for them to be able to make
appropriate decisions. The “unknown” value is not allowed to use according to the profile. The current time
plus the time it takes the traffic light controller to change the physical light is always true as minEndTime.
Therefore, an “unknown” value is not needed and shall not be used.
In actuated traffic light controllers, all the listed data elements for the time change details are estimated by
the infrastructure. There is no exact ground of truth against which the accuracy of the information can be
checked. Therefore, there is no requirement on accuracy for this information.
Traffic light controllers operating fixed time know the exact time of change, so it is useful to require a certain
accuracy in those cases. It is set to the allowed deviation of the system clock to the reference time. The chosen
ground of truth is the displayed traffic light since this was the most comprehensible and objective phrasing.
For testing purposes, the electrical controller output to the lights should be used as ground truth.
5.7.4.2 MaxEndTime
Refers to signalPhaseAndTimingMessage.intersections.states.state-time-speed.timing.maxEndTime
The data element maxEndTime consistutes the counterpart for minEndTime by providing the latest point in
time possible at which the respective phase state could change. For some actuated traffic lights, this might
not be known (e.g., when the phase changes only on pedestrian request). For that case, “out of range” is
always true as maximum end time and should be indicated – the C-Roads profile defines a dedicated value
for that purpose.
Same as for minEndTime the goal is to provide a correct representation of reality to receiving vehicles.
For traffic light controllers operating fixed time-the profile states that minEndTime and maxEndTime shall be
equal, thus indicating to receiving vehicles, that the time of change is known exactly.
5.7.4.3 LikelyTime
Refers to signalPhaseAndTimingMessage.intersections.states.state-time-speed.timing.likelyTime
Together with a comprehensible confidence indication, the likelyTime can provide a tendency for the most
likely time of change. Especially in cases when minEndTime and maxEndTime differ more than just a few
seconds, this can be of value for vehicle applications. For traffic lights that are only triggered by human

6. SPATEM basic interpretation of the
message
The “signal, phase and timing message” conveys information about the current status and signal phase state
of one or more signalized intersections.
This chapter describes how the data structures in SPATEM can – along with the corresponding reference to
MAPEM – be used to convey detailed information about the current and future signal phases as well as other
dynamic information for the intersection. For the interrelation between the two messages MAPEM and
SPATEM, see chapter 2.2.
6.1 Linking between SPATEM and MAPEM
As described before, the MAPEM provides receiving vehicles the information on the intersection topology,
including all lanes, how they are connected and which manoeuvres are possible.
The SPATEM is mainly linked to the MAPEM through their common intersection reference ID (see section
3.2.2) and revision (see section 3.2.3) so that vehicles can identify which SPATEM belongs to which MAPEM.
Another link between the two messages is the signal group identifier (see section 3.3.5.2). Every connection
in MAPEM managed by a traffic light has a signal group id assigned. In turn, every movement state in SPATEM
also has a unique signal group ID assigned. This way, the signal group identifier links MAPEM and SPATEM for
a specific manoeuvre. For each signal group, SPATEM provides information about the phase states and their
timing.
This way receiving vehicles can map the phase state information in SPATEM to the corresponding connection
of lanes in MAPEM and therefore to its own intended manoeuvre.
It is important to ensure that every signal group ID in MAPEM is described in the corresponding SPATEM. Also
the other way round, signal group ids described in SPATEM should also occur in MAPEM.
6.2 Representation of the time of signal change
The SPATEM also provides information about the time when the phases will change. This is not done in relative
numbers saying, “The signal will change in 15 s.” but rather in absolute time, saying “The signal will change at
17h:03m:20s.” The time format for this information is quite complex and is described in detail in section 5.7.4.
According to the current specifications, the time of signal change is represented by three elements: the
minimum end time, the maximum end time and the likely time. The formal definition and interplay of these
three elements is given in [ISO/TS 19091] and the C-Roads message profile [C-Roads MP]. This section at hand
provides some explanations for the requirements on minEndTime, maxEndTime and likelyTime as provided by
C-Roads.

(i.e., they should represent the true minimum and maximum. Figure 64 shows an exemplary sequence where
